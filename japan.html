Neighborhoods & areas

Shibuya & surroundings

A great home base for exploring. Miyashita Park and the Shibuya Scramble mall both offer solid views of the Scramble crossing, the busiest intersection in the world. For shopping, Loft and Tokyu Hands will eat up 3-4 hours with their endless quirky goods (Loft also has a MoMA store inside). From here you can walk to Harajuku for Meiji Jingumae shrine and Takeshita Street, or head to Yoyogi Park, which is Tokyo's answer to Central Park but much calmer. Omotesando, also nearby, has a Fifth Avenue feel with upscale boutiques lining a tree-lined boulevard.

Shopping Temples Parks Half day+
Shimokitazawa

Tokyo's thrifting capital. A bohemian neighborhood full of vintage shops, independent cafes, and small theaters tucked into narrow streets. Great if you want a break from the city's more polished areas.

Thrifting Chill vibes
Daikanyama

A lovely, walkable neighborhood between Shibuya and Ebisu. Home to Kinokuniya, arguably the best (and weirdest) bookstore in the world. It spans multiple buildings and is an experience in itself.

Kinokuniya
Nakameguro

The best spot in Tokyo for cherry blossoms when they're in bloom, with trees lining both sides of the Meguro River. One of only six Starbucks Reserve Roasteries in the world sits right along the water with views of the blossoms. Expect a multi-hour wait, so grab a queue ticket and explore the neighborhood while you wait. While you're in the area, Seirinkan serves some of the best Napolitan-style pizza you'll find anywhere.

Cherry blossoms Seirinkan pizza Starbucks Reserve
Ebisu

One of Tokyo's most livable neighborhoods, with an excellent yokocho (a narrow alley lined with small bars and eateries) and some of the city's best cocktail bars. Yebisu Garden Place has good shopping, and the observatory on the 39th floor offers free panoramic views.

Bars Free views
Asakusa

Sensoji temple is a must-see, and it's even better if you arrive by ferry from Odaiba rather than by train. Tokyo Skytree is a short walk away and offers some of the best panoramic city views you'll get.

Sensoji Ferry ride Skytree
Odaiba

Worth a half-day or full day. There's a giant Gundam statue, an artificial beach, tons of shopping, and solid food options. Fugetsu does great okonomiyaki, and there's a ramen lane where you can try bowls from different prefectures across Japan. TeamLab, the immersive digital art experience, is nearby and worth making time for.

Gundam TeamLab Ramen lane Half day+
Ginza

Tokyo's upscale district, but not just for luxury shopping. The world's largest Muji is here (along with a Muji hotel), plus Loft and Tokyu Hands. Ginza also has excellent cocktail bars, atmospheric yokochos, and a great soup curry spot.

Muji flagship Cocktail bars
Akihabara

The go-to for anime, manga, figurines, and electronics. Multi-story arcades, retro game shops, and maid cafes if you're curious. It's on the Yamanote line, so easy to reach from most neighborhoods.

Anime Electronics
Yokohama

The Gundam Factory features a moving, life-size Gundam that's worth the trip alone. Nearby, the Red Brick Warehouse has good shops and the Ramen Museum lets you sample styles from across the country. Don't miss Garlic Joe's, a specialty garlic restaurant in one of the malls that lives up to the hype.

Garlic Joe's Ramen Museum Life-size Gundam
Shinjuku

Shinjuku Gyoen has gorgeous gardens that feel miles from the city. A great place to slow down for an afternoon, especially during cherry blossom season.

Gardens
Avoid Roppongi

Extremely overhyped and a tourist trap, like Times Square. Locals stay away, and you should too.

Skip Golden Gai

It's become too touristy to enjoy properly. There are plenty of yokochos around the city (Ebisu, Ginza, and newer ones) that offer the same small-bar-alley experience without the crowds and markups.

02

Food

Ramen

Danbo's Rekka ramen at their Ebisu location is a standout, and noticeably better than their Seattle outpost. Afuri is the go-to for yuzu-based ramen, with locations in Ebisu, Shibuya, and Hiroo. Their yuzu tsukemen (broth and noodles served separately for dipping) is particularly good.

Danbo Afuri Ebisu
Yakiniku (grilled beef)

Japanese beef is on another level, and even chain restaurants like Toraji or Yakiniku Champion are excellent. No need to overthink this one or chase reservations at fancy spots.

Toraji Yakiniku Champion
Soup curry

Hard to find in the US, so take advantage while you're there. There's a great spot in Ginza, and if you make it up to Sapporo, that's the spiritual home of the dish.

Ginza Rare find
Sushi

Toyosu Market is the place for fresh sushi. Going early for a sushi breakfast there is one of those Tokyo experiences you won't forget.

Toyosu Market
Uni (sea urchin)

If you're at all open to it, try it here. Uni in Japan is a completely different experience from what you'll find stateside.

Must try
Okonomiyaki

Fugetsu in Odaiba is a good pick for this savory pancake/omelette hybrid. Fun to watch them make it on the griddle in front of you.

Odaiba
Pizza

Seirinkan in Nakameguro does Napolitan-style pizza that holds its own against anything in Italy. One of those places you wouldn't expect in Tokyo, but there it is.

Seirinkan
03

Experiences & attractions

Ghibli Museum

Located in Mitaka and worth the trip for any Ghibli fan. Tickets sell out fast, but third-party resellers usually have them, and the markup is worth paying. Book as early as you can.

Book aheadMitaka
TeamLab

Interactive, immersive digital art near Odaiba that's unlike anything else you'll experience. Rooms of light, water, and projections that respond to your movement. Make time for this one.

Must-do
Cherry blossoms

If they're in bloom during your visit, head to Nakameguro and walk along the Meguro River. The timing is everything with these, so check the forecast.

Seasonal
Day trips: Hakone & Nikko

Hakone is your best bet for Mt. Fuji views from the Tokyo side, while Nikko offers stunning shrines and a completely different atmosphere from the city. Both work as day trips and are well connected by train.

Fuji views Shrines Day trip
04

Cocktails & drinking

World-class cocktail bars

Tokyo has some of the world's best cocktail bars. They're a bit pricey but worth every yen. Bar Tram and Bar Trench in Ebisu are standouts, and Ginza has several worth seeking out. Look up a few lists and pick the ones that appeal to you.

Bar Tram Bar Trench Ebisu Ginza
Open container? No problem.

Japan has no open container laws, so you can walk and drink anywhere. You'll mostly see foreigners doing it, but it's not frowned upon. Grab a Strong Zero from any convenience store and enjoy the stroll.

Getting around

The Yamanote line

This loop line connects most neighborhoods in this guide and will quickly become your best friend. It hits Shibuya, Harajuku, Shinjuku, Ebisu, Akihabara, and many other stops mentioned here.

06

Other cities

Sapporo

Soup curry that might be the best dish in all of Japan, great sights, and fun winter activities. Worth the trip north.

Kobe

Museums, a distinctive harbor skyline, and world-famous Wagyu beef you'll be thinking about long after.

Kyoto

Castles, temples, and the famous Fushimi Inari shrine with its thousands of torii gates winding up the mountainside.

Nara

Known for the bowing deer that roam freely through the park. An easy and fun day trip from Kyoto.

Fukuoka

Birthplace of Hakata-style tonkotsu ramen and home to yatai (open-air food stalls) along the river. Laid-back energy.

Beppu

Hot spring capital of Japan. Volcanic steam rises everywhere, the caramel custard is to die for, and the onsens are world-class.

Kawaguchiko

The best views of Mt. Fuji, bar none. Try the local specialty Dango-jiru (thick noodle soup) while you're there.

07

Practical tips

Get a Pasmo or Suica card

Pick one up at any train station vending machine. It works for transit and at most convenience stores, vending machines, and many restaurants. Saves a lot of fumbling with cash.

Google Translate & Maps

Google Translate's camera mode is a lifesaver for menus that don't have English. Google Maps is excellent in Japan with detailed transit directions down to the platform number.

Carry a bag for trash

Public trashcans are rare. Keep a small bag with you for wrappers and bottles. Convenience stores like 7-Eleven and Family Mart usually have bins you can use, though.

Keep cash on hand

ATMs at Family Mart and 7-Eleven work with foreign cards. Many smaller places (especially ramen shops) only take cash via vending machine ordering. Afuri and Danbo both accept cards, though.
